Definitions

1. Geologic Principles

Principle of Superposition

In any sequence of undisturbed sedimentary rock layers, the oldest layers are at the bottom and the youngest are at the top. This principle helps geologists understand the chronological order of rock formations.

Principle of Original Horizontality

Sedimentary layers are generally deposited in a horizontal or nearly horizontal position due to gravity. If the layers are tilted or folded, it suggests they have been moved from their original position by geological forces.

Principle of Original Continuity

Layers of sediment extend laterally in all directions until they thin out or encounter a barrier. If a layer stops abruptly, it might have been eroded or disrupted after deposition.

Principle of Cross-Cutting Relationships

If a geological feature, such as a fault or an igneous intrusion, cuts through another rock layer, it must be younger than the layers it cuts. This helps geologists identify the relative ages of formations.

Principle of Inclusions

Fragments of rock (inclusions) found within another rock layer must be older than the rock layer containing them. This is because the inclusion had to exist before being incorporated into the surrounding material.

2. Unconformities

Unconformity

An unconformity is a surface that represents a period where no sediment was deposited or erosion removed layers, indicating a gap in the geologic record.

Angular Unconformity

Occurs when tilted or folded rock layers are overlain by younger, more horizontally layered rocks, indicating a past period of deformation and erosion before newer layers were deposited.

Nonconformity

This type of unconformity occurs when sedimentary rock layers lie on top of eroded igneous or metamorphic rock, showing that an older, crystalline rock was exposed and eroded before new sediment was deposited.

Disconformity

Found between parallel sedimentary rock layers, a disconformity signifies a time of non-deposition or erosion that disrupted continuous sedimentary accumulation.

3. Fossils and Dating

Fossil Succession

Fossils appear in a specific, recognizable order in the rock layers, allowing geologists to use fossils to date the relative ages of rocks based on this predictable pattern.

Index Fossil

An index fossil is from a species that was widespread, short-lived, and easily identifiable, making it ideal for dating the specific rock layer in which it’s found.
occupy short span of time and have large geographic range and are very useful for correlating strata
AMMONITES ARE IMPORTANT

Fossil Assemblage

A collection of fossils found together in a rock layer that helps establish the relative age.

Mass Number

The total number of protons and neutrons in an atom’s nucleus, giving insight into the isotope’s stability and weight top left.

Atomic Number

The number of protons in an atom’s nucleus, which determines the element’s identity and properties, bottom left.

Atomic Weight

The weighted average mass of an element’s naturally occurring isotopes, often expressed as the mass number.

Isotope(s)

Atoms of an element with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons, giving them different atomic masses.

Beta Decay: n -> p^+ + e^- (z+1)

A radioactive decay process where a neutron turns into a proton, emitting a beta particle, which changes the atom into a different element.

Electron Capture

p^+ + e^- -> n (z-1) In this decay process, an electron is absorbed by the nucleus, turning a proton into a neutron, often resulting in the formation of a new element.

Alpha Decay

Large unstable nucleus -> smaller more stable nucleus + alpha particle (A-4, Z-2)
A radioactive decay process where an atom emits an alpha particle (two protons and two neutrons), resulting in a new, lighter element.

Half-Life

The time it takes for half of a sample of a radioactive substance to decay, helping determine the age of rocks and fossils.

Closure Temperature

The temperature below which a mineral in a rock becomes closed to the exchange of isotopes, effectively "locking in" the isotopic composition for dating purposes.

Weathering Processes

Physical Weathering
The mechanical breakdown of rocks into smaller pieces without changing their chemical composition, often due to temperature changes, pressure release, or biological activity.
Joint
Naturally occurring cracks or fractures in rocks that provide pathways for weathering processes to penetrate and break down the rock.
joints are cracks in rocks, bedding plane are horizontal tectonic are vertical,
exfoliation joints are from unloading large, flat-lying, curved fractures in rock that run parallel to the Earth's surface.
Cooling joints hexagonal columns (Devils tower WY)
Exfoliation
The peeling or shedding of rock layers due to pressure release when overlying materials are removed, common in large rock masses.
Spheroidal Weathering
The process where rock corners and edges are rounded off as they weather faster than flat surfaces, often in humid environments.
Frost Wedging
Occurs when water enters rock cracks, freezes, and expands, causing the rock to fracture further.
Root Wedging
Plant roots grow into rock cracks, exerting pressure that forces the rock apart over time.
Salt Wedging
Salt crystals grow in rock pores or fractures, weakening the rock.
Chemical Weathering
The alteration of minerals within a rock through chemical reactions, often involving water and gases like oxygen or carbon dioxide.
Happens through dissolution often of limestone,
measured surface reduction over years
Hydrolysis
A chemical reaction between minerals and water that leads to the breakdown of minerals, often transforming them into new minerals.
example: Feldapar + water = clay
Oxidation
A reaction where minerals react with oxygen, especially in rocks with iron, which can form rust and weaken the rock.
Formation of hematite and other mafic minerals

Soil Types

Alfisol
A fertile, clay-rich soil typically found in humid regions, often supporting hardwood forests and agricultural use.
Aridisol
Soil that forms in arid climates with little organic material due to minimal vegetation, often containing salts.
Oxisol
Highly weathered soil rich in iron and aluminum, commonly found in tropical rainforests where intense weathering occurs.
Forms in tropical areas, iron-rich, no B horizon nothing leached accumulates, buildup of insoluble materials.
Laterite
A soil type with high iron and aluminum content, developing in tropical regions with heavy rainfall and intense chemical weathering.

Mass Wasting Types

Mass Wasting
The downslope movement of soil and rock under the influence of gravity, which can occur at various speeds and intensities.
Creep
A very slow movement of soil down a slope, often noticeable only over long periods.
Slump
A type of mass wasting where a mass of rock or soil moves down a slope in a rotational motion, often leaving a curved scar.
Earthflow
The flow of saturated soil and weathered rock downhill, typically after heavy rainfall.
Landslide
A rapid, often destructive movement of rock and soil down a steep slope, usually triggered by natural events like earthquakes or heavy rain. , occur when large masses of earth or rock move along relatively flat or gently inclined planes of weakness, such as bedding planes, joints, or fault lines.
These planes of weakness can form due to various geological processes, including weathering or tectonic activity.
Gravity is the main driving force, but factors like water infiltration, earthquakes, and human activity can reduce friction along these planes, making the slope unstable and triggering the slide.
slide blocks can be on exfoliation joint surfaces, shale beds, or foliation, when downslope force is stronger than resistance.
Sliding of less consolidated material in rotational slides
Mudflow
A fast-moving flow of mud, especially after intense rainfall in areas with loose soil.
Debris Flow
Similar to mudflow but includes larger rock fragments, often occurring in mountainous areas.
Rockslide
A sudden and rapid sliding of rock down a steep slope, often triggered by weathering or human activity.

Stream and River Terms

Watershed
An area where all the water drains into a single river or stream system, impacting water flow and sediment transport.
Water Collection: A watershed gathers all surface water (rain, snowmelt) and guides it towards rivers, streams, and lakes. This is essential for providing drinking water, irrigation, and industrial use.
A river profile refers to the cross-sectional view of a river from its source (the headwaters) to its mouth. It shows the shape, slope, and characteristics of the river along its entire course.
The gradient of a river is the slope or steepness of the riverbed over a specific distance
tributaries
Tributaries: Smaller streams or rivers that flow into a larger body of water (the main river or lake). They contribute additional water and nutrients to the system.
Stream Gradient
The slope or steepness of a stream, influencing the speed and energy of water flow.
Discharge
The volume of water flowing through a stream at a given point, affecting erosion and sediment movement.
m3/sec or ft3/sec
Q = V x A
Q = discharge m3/sec, V = current velocity m/s, A = Channel cross sectional area m2
Bed Load
Larger particles like sand and gravel transported along the bottom of a stream due to water flow.
Suspended Load
Fine sediment carried within the water column, often making the water appear murky. OVER 90% of total load
Dissolved Load
Minerals dissolved in stream water, invisible to the naked eye but important for nutrient transport.
Stream Profile
The change in elevation of a stream from its source to its mouth, influencing the stream’s energy and erosion patterns.
Base Level
The lowest point a stream can erode to, typically sea level for most rivers.
Knickpoint
A steep section in a stream profile, often resulting in features like waterfalls. Develop at resistant rocks or scarps, migrate upstream, Niagra falls (erosion occurs 1m/yr and it eroded 12km, therefore 12,000 years)
Stream Terrace
A flat area along a stream that represents an old floodplain, often elevated due to erosion.

Bar
An accumulation of sediment, like sand or gravel, within a river channel, often forming islands or shoals that can shift over time.

A river with pronounced, winding bends or loops, created as water erodes the outer banks of curves and deposits sediment on the inner banks.

Cut Bank
The outer edge of a river bend where erosion occurs due to faster-moving water, often forming a steep bank.
Point Bar
The inner edge of a river bend where slower-moving water deposits sediment, building up a gentle slope opposite the cut bank.
Oxbow Lake
A U-shaped lake formed when a river meander is cut off from the main channel, often as the result of erosion and sediment deposition.
Floodplain
The flat area surrounding a river, periodically flooded when the river overflows, depositing nutrient-rich sediment beneficial for agriculture.

Flood Control and Water Management

Flood Prevention and Control Features

Channelization
The process of altering a river’s course, often by straightening or deepening the channel, to improve water flow and reduce flood risk.
Retention Pond
A pond designed to hold runoff water, allowing it to be slowly released into nearby rivers or groundwater, helping to prevent floods and manage water quality.
Detention Basin
A temporary storage area for stormwater runoff, which holds excess water and releases it gradually to reduce downstream flooding.

Coastal Terms

Coastal Processes and Features

Karst
A landscape formed by the dissolution of soluble rocks, like limestone, resulting in features such as sinkholes, caves, and underground drainage.
Recreational Beach
A shoreline area maintained for public use, typically sandy, offering space for activities like swimming, sunbathing, and sports.
Beach (Entire Shoreface)
The zone extending from the sand dunes down to the water, including the foreshore and backshore, shaped by wave action and sediment movement.
Berm
A raised, flat section of the beach formed by wave-deposited sand, marking the highest reach of waves during high tide or storms.
Bar
An underwater ridge of sand or sediment near the shore, often parallel to the coastline, which can influence wave patterns and beach shape.
Longshore Current/Transport
The movement of sand and sediment along the coast, driven by waves hitting the shore at an angle, shaping beaches over time.
Inlet
A narrow waterway that connects bays, lagoons, or estuaries to the ocean, allowing tidal exchange and influencing local currents.
Tidal Delta
A sediment deposit formed at the mouth of an inlet due to tidal flows, often containing rich ecosystems.
Migrating Inlet
An inlet that shifts its position over time due to sediment movement and tidal action, impacting nearby landforms.
Breathing Inlet
An inlet that periodically closes and reopens, often due to sand accumulation and water flow changes.
Returning Inlet
An inlet that closes for a time but eventually reopens, maintaining a connection between the ocean and inland waters.
Shoreline Retreat
The landward movement of the shoreline over time, typically due to erosion from wave action or rising sea levels.
Groin
A structure built perpendicular to the shore to trap sand and reduce beach erosion, creating localized areas of sediment accumulation.
Jetty
A long structure extending into the ocean to protect a harbor entrance from sediment buildup, maintaining safe navigation channels.
Seawall
A barrier constructed along the coast to protect land from waves and prevent coastal erosion, often affecting natural beach dynamics.
