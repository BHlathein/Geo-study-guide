Geologic Time: Relative Dating

  • Relative vs. Absolute Dating: Relative dating places events in a sequential order without knowing the exact age, using methods such as comparing rock layers. Absolute dating, on the other hand, provides a specific age using radiometric techniques, measuring decay of isotopes to determine when events occurred.
  • Principles of Relative Dating by Steno: Fundamental principles used to understand rock formation and relationships:
    • Superposition: In undisturbed layers, the oldest layer lies at the bottom, and each successive layer above is younger.
    • Original Horizontality: Layers of sediments are originally deposited horizontally; tilting or folding indicates later geological events.
    • Cross-Cutting Relationships: Geological features like faults or intrusions are younger than the rock layers they cut through.
  • Unconformities: Surfaces indicating gaps in the geologic record, often due to erosion or non-deposition.
    • Disconformity: Represents a period of erosion or non-deposition between parallel sedimentary layers.
    • Nonconformity: Occurs between older igneous/metamorphic rocks and younger sedimentary layers on top.
    • Angular Unconformity: Tilted or folded sedimentary rocks overlain by younger, flat-lying layers, indicating past tectonic activity.
  • Determining Relative Ages: Geologists use principles like cross-cutting relationships and superposition to sequence rock layers, establishing the order in which events occurred.
  • Identifying Unconformities: Unconformities are identified by observing changes in rock continuity, such as gaps or erosional surfaces in diagrams or rock samples.
  • Fossils and Relative Dating: Fossils provide clues for relative dating by correlating rock layers with known fossil assemblages.
    • Index fossils: Distinctive, widespread fossils limited to a particular time range, which helps in dating the rocks in which they’re found.

Geologic Time: Absolute Dating

  • Subscripts and Superscripts: In isotope notation, superscripts indicate the atomic mass (total protons + neutrons), while subscripts represent the atomic number (number of protons).
  • Atomic Number, Mass Number, Atomic Weight, and Isotopes: Key terms to understand atomic structure:
    • Atomic Number: Number of protons in the nucleus, determining the element.
    • Mass Number: Total number of protons and neutrons, indicating an isotope's mass.
    • Atomic Weight: Weighted average of an element’s isotopes based on natural abundance.
    • Isotopes: Variants of the same element with different numbers of neutrons; radioactive isotopes decay over time, useful for dating.
  • Radioactive Decay: Process where unstable isotopes lose energy by emitting particles; critical in radiometric dating for age determination.
  • Types of Radioactive Decay: Different decay mechanisms release particles or energy:
    • Alpha Decay: Emits two protons and two neutrons, reducing atomic number by 2 and mass by 4 (e.g., Uranium-238 to Thorium-234).
    • Beta Decay: Neutron converts to a proton, increasing atomic number by 1 with no mass change (e.g., Carbon-14 to Nitrogen-14).
    • Gamma Decay: Release of high-energy photons without changing atomic structure.
  • Half-Life: The time it takes for half of a sample of radioactive isotope to decay, a critical concept in calculating the ages of rocks and fossils.
  • Dating Methods: Common radiometric dating methods include:
    • Uranium-Lead Dating: Effective for dating ancient igneous rocks.
    • Potassium-Argon Dating: Useful for dating volcanic rocks older than 100,000 years.
    • Carbon-14 Dating: Best for dating organic materials up to about 50,000 years old.
  • C-14 vs. Other Methods: Carbon-14 is specific to recent, organic materials, unlike other methods suited to much older, inorganic rocks.
  • Assumptions in Radiometric Dating: Relies on constant decay rates, closed systems, and an accurate measure of initial daughter elements.
  • Dating Events: Radiometric dating records events like rock crystallization or metamorphic reformation, not the time the rocks were originally formed.
  • Closed System Viability: Preservation of a closed system is key for accurate dating; factors like weathering or metamorphism can alter isotopic ratios.

Weathering and Soil

  • Chemical vs. Physical Weathering: Physical weathering breaks rocks into smaller pieces without altering their composition, while chemical weathering changes mineral composition.
  • Types of Weathering: Examples include:
    • Physical Weathering: Frost wedging (water freezing in cracks), unloading (reduction in pressure on rocks), and thermal expansion (temperature changes).
    • Chemical Weathering: Oxidation (reaction with oxygen), hydrolysis (reaction with water), and dissolution (soluble minerals dissolve in water).
  • Unloading/Exfoliation Joints: Form when overlying pressure decreases, allowing rock to expand and crack, typically in granite.
  • Cooling Joints: Form in cooling igneous rocks as they contract, often creating hexagonal columns in rocks like basalt.
  • Assisting Mechanisms: Physical weathering increases surface area, accelerating chemical reactions; chemical weathering weakens rock structure, aiding physical breakdown.
  • Soil Profile Formation: Soil develops distinct layers called horizons:
    • O Horizon: Organic material layer at the surface.
    • A Horizon: Topsoil, rich in organic and mineral material.
    • E Horizon: lighter colored, significantly leached.
    • B Horizon: Subsoil, accumulation zone for minerals leached from above.
    • C Horizon: Predominately bedrock.
  • Soil Types: Different soils form in varying climates:
    • Alfisol: Temperate climate, medium thickness, organic rich A-horizon, humus+leached soil, iron oxide accumulate calcite is leached.
    • Aridisol: Develops in dry climates, thin, build up of soluble material like calcite significant leaching, well developed on limestone.
    • Oxisol: Forms in tropical areas, iron-rich, no B horizon nothing leached accumulates, buildup of insoluble materials.

Mass Wasting

  • Definition: Mass wasting is the movement of soil, rock, and debris downslope due to gravity. It includes landslides, mudflows, and slumps.
  • Types of Mass Wasting: Classified by movement type and material:
    • Falls: Occur when rock breaks loose and falls freely or bounces down a slope.
    • Slides: Movement of material along a well-defined surface, such as a rockslide on a cliff face.
    • Flows: Material behaves more like a fluid; debris flows, mudflows, and earthflows are common examples.
    • Slumps: Occur when a block of material moves along a curved surface, leaving a scarp at the top.
  • Factors Influencing Mass Wasting: Key elements include:
    • Water Content: Saturation of soil and rock can weaken materials and promote sliding.
    • Slope Angle: Steeper slopes are more prone to mass wasting due to gravity's effect.
    • Vegetation: Roots stabilize slopes; removal can increase mass wasting risk.
    • Human Activity: Construction, mining, and deforestation can destabilize slopes.
  • Preventative Measures: Techniques to reduce mass wasting risks include retaining walls, slope grading, drainage control, and afforestation.

Streams and Rivers

  • Stream Processes: Streams erode, transport, and deposit sediments, shaping landscapes over time.
  • Stream Erosion: Streams erode their beds and banks through hydraulic action, abrasion, and solution, widening and deepening channels.
  • Stream Transportation: Methods include:
    • Suspended Load: Fine particles (e.g., silt and clay) are carried in suspension by turbulent water.
    • Bed Load: Larger particles (e.g., sand and gravel) roll, slide, or bounce along the riverbed.
    • Dissolved Load: Soluble materials are carried in solution within the water.
  • Stream Deposition: As streams slow down, they deposit sediment, forming features like alluvial fans, deltas, and levees.
  • Stream Types: Based on gradient and sediment load:
    • Meandering Streams: Develop sinuous curves, or meanders, and typically form in flat areas.
    • Braided Streams: Characterized by multiple channels that split and rejoin, often carrying high sediment loads.
  • Drainage Patterns: The pattern a stream system follows, influenced by underlying geology, includes dendritic, radial, rectangular, and trellis patterns.

Flooding

  • Floods: Occur when water levels rise and overflow riverbanks, often due to heavy rainfall, rapid snowmelt, or storm surges.
  • Types of Floods: Different types are influenced by factors like location and cause:
    • Flash Floods: Rapid, short-duration floods resulting from intense rainfall over a small area.
    • River Floods: Caused by prolonged rainfall over large areas, leading to rivers overflowing.
    • Coastal Floods: Occur due to storm surges, high tides, or tsunamis along coastlines.
  • Flood Recurrence Interval: The average time between floods of a particular size; a 100-year flood has a 1% chance of occurring in any given year.
  • Flood Control Methods: Include levees, dams, and diversion channels to manage water flow and reduce flood risk.
  • Floodplain: The flat land adjacent to a river, subject to flooding; it often has fertile soil due to deposited sediments.

Groundwater

  • Groundwater Storage and Movement: Water infiltrates the ground and fills porous spaces in soil and rock, moving through aquifers.
  • Aquifers: Permeable rock layers that store and transmit groundwater. Two main types:
    • Unconfined Aquifer: Open to the surface, recharged directly by precipitation.
    • Confined Aquifer: Bounded by impermeable layers, under pressure, often recharged far from where it’s accessed.
  • Water Table: The boundary between saturated and unsaturated zones; it fluctuates with precipitation and groundwater extraction.
  • Porosity and Permeability: Porosity is the volume of pore space in rock or sediment, while permeability is the ability to transmit water. High porosity doesn’t always mean high permeability.
  • Wells and Artesian Systems: Wells tap into groundwater; artesian wells flow naturally due to pressure in a confined aquifer.
  • Groundwater Contamination: Pollutants from sources like agriculture, industry, or landfills can degrade water quality in aquifers.

Coastal Processes

  • Wave Action: Waves erode, transport, and deposit sediment along coastlines, shaping shorelines over time.
  • Coastal Erosion: Waves and currents erode beaches and cliffs; factors include wave energy, rock type, and coastal slope.
  • Longshore Drift: Process by which sediment moves along the shore, driven by wave action at an angle to the coastline.
  • Tides: Periodic rise and fall of sea levels caused by gravitational interactions between the Earth, moon, and sun.
    • Spring Tides: Occur during full and new moons, with higher high tides and lower low tides.
    • Neap Tides: Occur during quarter moons, with less extreme high and low tides.
  • Shoreline Features: Created by coastal processes:
    • Beaches: Sandy or pebbly shorelines formed by sediment deposition.
    • Barrier Islands: Long, narrow islands parallel to the coast, protecting shorelines from waves.
    • Spits: Narrow extensions of sand that project from the shore into the water.
    • Sea Arches and Sea Stacks: Erosion of headlands forms arches; when arches collapse, isolated stacks remain.
  • Human Impact on Coastal Processes: Development, seawalls, and groins affect natural coastal erosion and sediment transport.
  • Coastal Management: Includes beach nourishment, artificial reefs, and shoreline armoring to mitigate erosion and protect infrastructure.
